Stellar Population Synthesis

What does the spectrum of a galaxy contain?

The spectrum of a galaxy can be divided into several different parts, represented by the following equation:

\[ F_{obs}(\lambda) = \sum_i \left[ w_i e^{-\tau\left(\frac{\lambda}{1+z}\right)}S_i\left(\frac{\lambda}{1+z}\right) \right] + F_{\text{emission}}\left(\frac{\lambda}{1+z}\right) + F_{\text{AGN}}\left(\frac{\lambda}{1+z}\right) \]

In this equation:

  • \( F_{obs}(\lambda) \) represents the observed flux of the galaxy as a function of wavelength \( \lambda \).
  • \( S_i\left(\frac{\lambda}{1+z}\right) \) denotes the intrinsic spectrum of individual stellar populations, adjusted for the redshift \( z \) of the galaxy. The index \( i \) sums over different stellar population components.
  • \( w_i \) are the weight factors representing the relative contributions of each stellar population component to the total spectrum.
  • \( e^{-\tau\left(\frac{\lambda}{1+z}\right)} \) accounts for the effects of dust extinction, where \( \tau \) is the optical depth as a function of wavelength, also corrected for redshift.
  • \( F_{\text{emission}}\left(\frac{\lambda}{1+z}\right) \) represents the contribution from emission lines, which may originate from ionized gas within the galaxy, such as HII regions or supernova remnants.
  • \( F_{\text{AGN}}\left(\frac{\lambda}{1+z}\right) \) is the component representing non-stellar radiation from an active galactic nucleus, if present.

The observed spectrum, \( F_{obs}(\lambda) \), thus consists of the combination of stellar light, dust extinction effects, emission lines, and possible AGN contributions, all appropriately adjusted for the galaxy's redshift. By fitting this model to observed data, we can infer the physical properties of the galaxy, such as its stellar population age distribution, metallicity, dust content, and the presence of AGN activity.
